History
Dissociative Identity Disorder has come a long way since the first encounter of the disorder in 1971. As the disorder was studied, many new theories have come up explaining the phenomena, but we have a long way to go before we can permanently eradicate the disorder from humanity.

1791

In 1791, A 20-year-old German woman reported that she became a different woman at times. At times she would start to speak perfect French, behave like a French Aristocrat, and spoke German with a French accent. When she was the French woman, she remembered everything, but as the German woman she did not remember the acts and thoughts of the French woman.

1880 - 1920

The reports of DID exploded during 1880 - 1920. Over 67% of all reported case of the disorder was diagnosed during this time frame. After 1920, the reports fell, as Freud found another disorder called Schizophrenia.

20th Century

In 1970, the popular book Sybil increased the amount of diagnosis of DID. 1970 had more reports of DID than all of the reports since 1816. From 1991 to 1997, there were over 500 cases of DID in one treatment center in Dallas Texas. As the amount of reports increased, so did the amount of personalities in each case. The majority of cases noted by 1944 manifested with only two personalities, but by 1997, cases averaged around 15 to 16 personalities.

DSM
The DSM (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders) is a manual published by the American Psychiatric Association containing a list of psychological disorders and offers criteria for the diagnosis of such disorders. The DSM has changed many times as new research has been found on psychological disorders.  you may click on the images to learn more. DSM I and DSM II

The DSM-I, in 1952, included DID as psychoneurotic disorders, in which anxiety is either "directly felt and expressed or . . . unconsciously and automatically controlled" by various defense mechanisms. 
​
The DSM-II, in 1968, classified DID as hysterical neurosis, dissociative type and was defined as an alteration to consciousness and identity.

DSM III and DSM IV

The DSM-III, in 1980, characterized "dissociative" as a separate class of disorders. It said DID is "a disturbance in the normally integrative functions of identity, memory, or consciousness . . ." This liberal definition leads to the increase in cases during its time.​

The DSM-IV, in 1994, the criterion for DID did not changed much, but included a specific criterion of amnesia and changed the name from multiple personality disorder to DID.
